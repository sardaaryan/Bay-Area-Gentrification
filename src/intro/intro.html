The Bay Area Gentrification Crisis

How the San Francisco Bay Area became the most expensive region in the United States

The Problem

The San Francisco Bay Area is a vibrant region of our great country, and one of the most economically fertile metropolitan areas in the entire world. It is also the source of arguably the worst gentrification and housing shortage in the United States of America, outstripping even places such as Manhattan and Los Angeles, in housing costs and socioeconomic disparity.

Gentrification is complex, and while there is no scholarly consensus on it’s exact definition, it is generally understood as the process of wealthier individuals moving into lower-income neighborhoods, causing displacement of local residents, increased property value, costlier rents, and a shift in community culture. Indelibly, this leads to a housing shortage, as the cost of buying a home becomes too unaffordable for median income buyers. In the case of the Bay Area, this shortage is vast. The presence of these phenomena in the bay have a shared cause: Silicon Valley.

Technological innovation around the world, and the computer science industry at large, can be traced back to Silicon Valley in the Bay Area. Indeed, it is still the greatest tech hub in the world. Although this is a source of great pride, it is also the predominant cause of gentrification in the Bay Area, as highly paid engineers oust the local residents from the market. As such, as of 2025, it is the most expensive place to live in the United States.

This is a significant problem that has its roots in the mid-90s, as Silicon Valley began to situate itself in San Jose. This visualization focuses on contemporary gentrification, from the year 2010 to the year 2023.

Measuring Gentrification

Measuring gentrification is complicated. This article operationalizes how to measures gentrification into 5 attributes, which have been acquired from the 2010-2023 American Community Survey (ACS). The 5 attributes, and how to calculate gentrification with them, is tabulated below:

Gentrification Criteria to Quantify Tracts
ACS Variable Criteria for Gentrification Census Variable Name
Median household income If the tract's percentage increase is above the county's percentage increase. S1901
Median home value If a tract’s percentage increase for home value is higher than the countywide median percentage increase in home value. S2506
Gross rent If a tract’s percentage is higher than the countywide percentage increase in rent cost. B25064
Occupancy Rate If the percentage decrease is above the county's percentage decrease. B25002
Educational attainment Percentage increase in college educated residents is above the countywide percentage increase in college educated residents. S1501
A few tweaks have been incorporated for measurement of this criteria:
  • Percentage change is compared on a year-to-year basis for each attribute.
  • The gentrification criteria in the article performs this logic on a citywide basis. We've elected to do countywide for simpler JSON representation.
  • Gentrification is not heterogeneous in our visualization. For the heatmap, levels of gentrification is mapped to a color gradient based on a baseline.

And thus we arrive at a formula for computing a "coefficient of gentrification" for a given year, to allow the heatmap to really shine. A tract is considered gentrified for the year if:

  1. \(\forall X_n \text{, where} ~ n = {1, 2, 3, 4, 5}\)
  2. \( X_n \) corresponds to attribute n for the census tract for the given year
  3. \(x_n\) corresponds to attribute n for the census tract for the previous year
  4. \( \overline{X}_n\) is the county average for attribute n in the current year
  5. \(\overline{x}_n\) is the county average for attribute n in the previous year
  6. \( G_n = \frac{X_n}{x_n} \)
  7. \( \overline{G}_n = \frac{\overline{X}_n}{\overline{x}_n} \)
  8. \( \text{if} ~ \frac{G_n}{\overline{G}_n} > 1 ~ \text{, then n is a gentrifiable attribute for the census tract} \)
  9. \( \text{The census tract is gentrifiable if and only if all n attributes are gentrifiable} \)
  10. The measure of gentrification can be determined as the average of gentrifying attributes, or \( M = \frac{\Sigma_{i = 1}^n~\frac{G_i}{\overline{G}_i}}{5} \). This holds only if all attributes are gentrifying by the above steps.

Note this is slightly different for educational attainment, which is a decrease in percentage, so this attribute \(n\) is a gentrifying attribute if \( \frac{G_n}{\overline{G}_n} < 1 \)

The year 2010 is our baseline, which means it will not have any measured gentrification. You are encouraged to start at this year for a county, and see how it changes over time into the year 2023. The value M thus measures the intensity of gentrification, which is mapped to the colorscale, but only if all 5 attributes are gentrifiying. By removing the binary label of "gentrified" or "non-gentrified", the hope is to indicate socioeconomic disparity at a higher granularity.

A 6th attribute, population, is also measured across census tracts. While it does not correlate to gentrification, it does loosely correlate to residential displacement, which is a typical side-affect of a gentrifying neighborhood.
